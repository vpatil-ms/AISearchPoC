Vermont has enacted legislation (Senate Bill 103), which expands protections under the state's current equal pay, nondiscrimination and antiharassment laws and limits certain no-rehire provisions. Senate Bill 103 is effective immediately.

The Details

Pay Protections

Senate Bill 103 amends Vermont's equal pay law, which previously only protected against pay discrimination on the basis of sex. The amended law adds additional protection on the basis of race, national origin, sexual orientation, gender identity or an employee who is a qualified individual with a disability.

The law does not:

  • Create new employer rights to ask about an employee's protected characteristic that is otherwise unknown to the employer; or
  • Diminish an employee's right to privacy under any law, applicable contract or collective bargaining agreement.

Rehire Clauses

The amended law prohibits agreements to settle a claim of employment discrimination from including a provision that prevents the employee from working for the employer or an affiliate of the employer in the future.

Discrimination and Harassment

The law also makes it easier to prove that discrimination or harassment occurred. For example, under the amended law, except when an employee alleges pay discrimination or disparate impact discrimination (e.g., where policies, practices or rules disproportionately impact a protected group), an employee is not required to identify another employee to whom the employee's treatment can be compared for purposes of showing that unlawful discrimination occurred.

Additionally, the amended law makes clear that harassment does not need to be severe or pervasive (a federal law standard) to be considered unlawful under Vermont law.

Under the law:

  • A single incident may constitute unlawful harassment;
  • Multiple incidents may be considered as harassment, and they will be considered in the aggregate with varying types of conduct and conduct based on multiple characteristics viewed in totality (rather than in isolation); and
  • An employee's conduct may be unlawful unless a reasonable employee with the same protected characteristic would consider the conduct a petty slight or trivial inconvenience.

Senate Bill 103 also amends the definition of sexual harassment to be a form of sex discrimination and means unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors and other verbal or physical, written, auditory, or visual conduct (previously, only verbal or physical was listed under the law) of a sexual nature when:

  • Submission to the conduct is made (explicitly or implicitly) a term or condition of employment;
  • An individual's submission to (or rejection of) the conduct negatively impacts employment decisions pertaining to the individual; or
  • The conduct has the purpose or effect of interfering (the law previously required substantial interference) with an individual's work (previously “work performance”) or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.

Review the amended law for more details.

Next Steps

  • Review existing policies, practices and sexual harassment prevention training to ensure compliance with Senate Bill 103.
  • Train supervisors on the requirements under the law.

The State of Washington has released information to assist employers in their requirements under the Washington Cares Act.

Background

Washington's long-term care benefits program, “WA Cares,” was set to begin on January 1, 2022. Under the program, employers were to deduct a .58 percent premium from employee wages and remit that premium to Washington's Employment Security Department (“ESD”).

WA Cares covered all employees employed in Washington State except certain collective bargaining employees, self-employed workers, and individuals who bought private long-term insurance to qualify for a one-time limited exemption.

In November of 2021, a lawsuit (Pacific Bells, et al. v. Inslee) was filed, stating that WA Cares violated ERISA and other federal laws. The case was dismissed, but to address concerns, Washington passed House Bill 1732 and House Bill 1733, which:

  • Delayed premium collections to July 1, 2023;
  • Delayed benefits to July 1, 2026;
  • Provides limited relief for employees within 10 years of retirement; and
  • Expands exemptions for certain employees.

The Details

Washington has released an employer toolkit, a FAQs sheet and more employer resources on its website to assist employers in preparing for WA Cares requirements.

Employer requirements

Starting July 1, 2023, employers must withhold a “premium” of .58 percent of an employee's wages unless the employee provided them with a state-issued exemption letter.

An employer must stop withholding from the employee by the effective date of the letter (or immediately if provided after the effective date) once it is received. Otherwise, the employer is liable to the employee. Washington will not return funds that are improperly withheld.

Note: The .58 percent premium is intended to be a state tax on employee wages, not a premium. The law caps the tax rate at .58 percent of wages, which cannot be increased without legislative action.

Employer requirements

Eligible wages

Wages that are subject to the tax are uncapped. All wages and remuneration (stock-based compensation, bonuses, paid time off, severance pay, etc.) are subject to the tax.

Benefits

Benefits will be payable on July 1, 2026. An eligible individual deemed by the Department of Social and Health Services to require assistance with at least three activities of daily living will receive up to $100 per day (up to a maximum lifetime limit of $36,500).

WA Cares benefit qualification requirements

Washington residents will receive full benefits if they have paid the tax for:

  • Ten years without interruption of five or more consecutive years; or
  • Three of the last six years before applying for the benefits

WA Cares benefit qualification requirements

Employees born before January 1, 1968, who have not satisfied these requirements will receive a pro-rated 10% of the benefit amount for each year the tax is paid if they contributed to WA Cares for at least one year and worked at least 500 hours in that year.

Employees subject to the tax

Employees must pay the WA Cares tax if their service is located in Washington or they perform some services in Washington that are directed or controlled from Washington (if the service is not localized in any state).

Note: Employees subject to Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) will be subject to WA Cares.

Exceptions: The tax does not apply to self-employed workers, federal employees, and employees that qualify for an exemption.

Self-employed worker opt-in option

Self-employed workers are automatically exempt from WA Cares. To participate in the program, they must opt in no later than July 1, 2026, or within three years of becoming self-employed for the first time. Once the option is elected, they cannot withdraw from coverage.

Employees exempt from the tax

Workers that qualify for an exemption and want to opt out of the program (except self-employed workers) must apply to and receive an exemption from ESD and provide the state-issued exemption letter to their employer.

The following employees may apply to ESD for an exemption:

  • An employee of an employer in Washington who has a permanent address outside of Washington as their primary residence;
  • An employee who holds a nonimmigrant visa for temporary workers, under federal law, and is employed by an employer in Washington;
  • A veteran who is rated by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs as having a service connected disability of 70 percent or greater (they receive a permanent exemption); or
  • A spouse or registered domestic partner of an active-duty service member in the United States armed forces (deployed or stationed outside of Washington).

An employee must notify the ESD and their employer within 90 days of a status change that causes them to no longer meet the exemption requirements. Failure to provide timely notice will result in the employee being liable for the premium and interest.

Note: The exemption period expired for most workers. Employees that bought private long-term care insurance by November 1, 2021, and applied for an exemption by December 31, 2022, should have received their exemption letters and provided them to their employers.

Next Steps

With WA Cares contributions scheduled to take effect on July 1, 2023, Washington employers should prepare to withhold wages and remit the WA Cares tax to ESD following the same means used for PFML contributions.

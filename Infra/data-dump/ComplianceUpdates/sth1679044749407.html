Tennessee has enacted legislation (House Bill 1853), which amends its Lawful Employment Act (the Act) to require employers to take certain actions regarding a candidate's work authorization status. House Bill 1853 takes effect on January 1, 2023.

Background:

Under federal law, employers are required to verify that each new hire is authorized to work in the United States by completing a Form I-9. Employers are prohibited from knowingly hiring or continuing to employ individuals who are not authorized to work.

The federal government developed an online electronic verification system known as “E-Verify,” which is a free Internet-based system operated by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) in partnership with the Social Security Administration. E-Verify allows employers to verify the employment eligibility of newly hired employees by comparing the information provided on the employee's Form I-9 to DHS databases. Currently, there is no federal requirement for private employers to use E-Verify, but some states have E-Verify mandates.

Tennessee E-Verify Requirements:

Covered Employers:

The Act lowers the threshold of employees that is used to determine whether an employer must use the federal E-Verify system from 50 to 35 full time equivalent employees beginning January 1, 2023.

Verification Assistance Provisions:

The law allows the state's office of employment verification assistance to help employers who do not have internet access, or those with fewer than 35 full time equivalent employees, to:

  • Enroll, at no charge, in the federal E-Verify program or conduct work authorization status checks; or
  • Conduct work authorization status checks of the employer's employees by using the E-Verify program, so long as the employer signs a prescribed form that attests to their qualification for assistance and completes paperwork required by the E-Verify program to permit the office to provide the assistance.

The law also clarifies that an employer is not liable for violating the Act during times in which the E-Verify program is suspended or not operational or if the employer receives and acts on a false employee report from the federal E-Verify program.

Illegal Alien Provisions:

The Act, which already prohibits an employer from knowingly employing, recruiting or referring (for a fee) an illegal alien, is amended to also prohibit knowingly rehiring an illegal alien.

Recordkeeping:

The law requires employers to maintain an E-Verify case result for each employee that shows that the employee is authorized to work through either the:

  • E-Verify Quick Audit Report;
  • E-Verify User Audit Report; or
  • Individual employee E-Verify case verification result.

Note: The E-Verify case result must be visible, showing the work authorization status.

Employer Protections:

Employees generally have the right to bring an action for wrongful discharge and national origin discrimination under the Tennessee Human Rights Act, among other things.

However, under the law, an employee cannot sue for retaliatory discharge or national origin discrimination if the employer terminates the employee because the employer discovers through the federal E-Verify program that the employee is not authorized to work in the United States and the employer was unaware of the employee's work status.

Next Steps:

Tennessee employers should review their hiring procedures and verify new hires through the E-Verify program beginning January 1, 2023.

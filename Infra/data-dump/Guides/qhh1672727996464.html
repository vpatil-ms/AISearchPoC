OSHA enforces its regulations by inspecting workplaces, issuing citations, and imposing monetary penalties for violations. Federal OSHA compliance officers will not normally make inspections unless there is due cause, such as a referral by an employee. However, local OSHA representatives can, and do, make unannounced inspections.

When scheduling OSHA inspections, the administration's priorities are as follows:

  • Investigations of imminent dangers. OSHA inspectors will come when there are any conditions or practices that could reasonably be expected to cause death or serious physical harm to employees, either immediately or before enforcement action can be taken. If a compliance officer finds an imminent danger situation, he or she will ask the employer to voluntarily stop the hazard and remove endangered employees from exposure.
  • Investigations of fatalities and catastrophes. These are any work-related incidents that result in an employee's death or the in-patient hospitalization of three or more employees. Such incidents must be reported to the nearest OSHA office by telephone or in person within eight (8) hours of the incident. OSHA investigates to determine the cause of these accidents and whether existing OSHA standards were violated.
  • Investigations of complaints. Complaints regarding a workplace hazard can come from any source. All employees have the right to request an OSHA inspection when there is reason to believe there is imminent danger due to a hazard or a violation of an OSHA standard. If requested, OSHA will maintain confidentiality, and will inform the employee of subsequent action taken.
  • Inspections that are programmed. These inspections are regularly scheduled in “high hazard” industries. Some small businesses and companies in low hazard industries are exempt from programmed inspections.

Inspection procedures:

OSHA compliance officers will familiarize themselves with any relevant facts prior to the visit, such as the employer's inspection history, the nature of the business, and the particular standards that might apply.

An inspection begins when the OSHA compliance officer arrives at the establishment. The inspector will explain the purpose of the visit, the scope of inspection as well as procedures for the walkthrough of the worksite. OSHA welcomes, but does not require, that there be an employee representative for each inspection. It is beneficial for the individual(s) who are most knowledgeable about company safety measures to accompany the OSHA officer; however, the compliance officer may interview employees privately during the course of the inspection.

OSHA compliance officers observe safety and health conditions and practices by performing the following tasks:

  • Consulting with employees privately, if necessary;
  • Taking photos, videotapes, and instrument readings;
  • Examining posting and records;
  • Collecting air samples;
  • Measuring noise levels;
  • Surveying existing engineering controls;
  • Monitoring employee exposure to toxic fumes, gases, and dust
  • Requesting a copy of the employer's Hazard Communication Program

The OSHA officer will point out any unsafe or unhealthful working conditions and discuss possible corrective action. Some violations can be corrected immediately. In these circumstances, the compliance officer records such corrections to help in judging the employer's good faith in compliance. Although corrected, the violations will serve as the basis for a citation and, if appropriate, a notice of proposed penalty. However, the penalties may be reduced.

When the walkthrough is complete, the compliance officer will give the employer and other involved parties a copy of Employer Rights and Responsibilities Following an OSHA Inspection. The compliance officer reviews all unsafe or unhealthful conditions and indicates apparent violations for which he or she may issue or recommend a citation and a proposed penalty. The OSHA inspector will not indicate any specific proposed penalties, but will inform the employer of their appeal rights. The employer may wish to produce records to show compliance efforts and information to help OSHA determine the time needed to abate an alleged violation.

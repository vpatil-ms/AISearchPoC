The following is an overview of some of the ACA's major insurance reforms. These provisions affect all employers who offer group health coverage, regardless of employer size. Keep these requirements in mind when evaluating your plan and working with your insurer on plan design.

Essential Health Benefits:

The ACA requires that certain non-grandfathered group health plans (see the Grandfathered Plans section for more information on grandfathered and non-grandfathered plans) offer a standard package of benefits referred to as “essential health benefits.” At a minimum, these benefits must include:

  • Ambulatory patient and emergency services;
  • Hospitalization;
  • Maternity and newborn care;
  • Mental health, behavioral health and substance use disorder services;
  • Prescription drugs;
  • Rehabilitative and habilitative services and devices;
  • Laboratory services;
  • Preventive and wellness services and chronic disease management; and
  • Pediatric services including oral and vision care.

Preventive Care:

“Recommended” preventive services and “routine” immunizations must be covered without subjecting participants to cost-sharing measures, such as a co-pay, deductible, or co-insurance, when such services are delivered by an in-network provider. This requirement does not apply to grandfathered health plans.

Preventive Services:

Some of the preventive services that must be covered include, but are not limited to:

  • Blood pressure, diabetes, and cholesterol tests;
  • Certain cancer screenings, including mammograms and colonoscopies;
  • Health counseling on issues such as quitting smoking, losing weight, eating healthfully, treating depression, and reducing alcohol use;
  • Routine vaccinations against diseases such as measles, polio, or meningitis;
  • Flu and pneumonia shots;
  • Counseling, screening, and vaccines to ensure healthy pregnancies; and
  • Regular well-baby and well-child visits, from birth to age 21

As new services are approved, health plans will generally be required to cover them with no cost-sharing for plan years beginning the following year. A full list of covered services is available on the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) website.

Routine Immunizations:

Health plans must also cover, without cost-sharing, routine vaccines that appear on the Immunization Schedules of the CDC. The CDC's Immunization Schedules can be found here.

Primary Care Physicians:

The ACA requires enrollees to be able to choose any available participating primary care provider as their doctor and available participating pediatrician as their child's primary care doctor. Women must be permitted to see OB-GYNs without a referral.

Annual & Lifetime Limit Prohibition:

  • Lifetime Limits: Health plans are prohibited from imposing lifetime limits on the essential benefits an individual may receive.
  • Annual Limits: The law also phased out the use of annual limits on essential benefits.

Nondiscrimination Rules:

Group health plans are prohibited from offering more generous benefits to highly compensated employees, such as shorter waiting periods for eligibility or lower employee contributions. Additional guidance is expected from the Treasury Department and the IRS, as well as the Departments of Labor and Health and Human Services on how nondiscrimination rules will be applied to group health plans.

Prohibiting Pre-Existing Condition Exclusions:

Group health plans are prohibited from limiting or denying benefits or denying coverage simply because an individual has a “pre-existing condition,” that is, a medical condition that developed before the individual applied for coverage. The ban on pre-existing condition exclusions applies to both grandfathered and non-grandfathered group health plans.

Dependent Coverage until Age 26:

There is no requirement for employer-sponsored health plans to offer dependent coverage, but if they do, the ACA requires they permit children to stay on their parents' health plan until they reach the age of 26. The requirement applies to both grandfathered and non-grandfathered health plans.

Changes to Rules for FSA, HRAs, and HSAs:

Health flexible spending account (FSA) salary reductions are limited to $2,550 per year. The $2,550 limit applies only to salary reduction contributions under a health care FSA and does not limit the amount permitted for reimbursement under an FSA for dependent care assistance or adoption care assistance.

Prescription Required:

Expenses incurred for a medicine or drug may be paid or reimbursed by an employer-provided FSA or HRA only if: (1) the drug requires a prescription; (2) the drug is available without a prescription and the individual obtains a prescription; or (3) the drug is insulin. A similar rule applies to HSAs and Archer Medical Savings Accounts.

Limits on Annual Cost Sharing:

In 2015, the maximum out of pocket cost sharing amount (deductibles, copayments and other amounts, excluding premiums) for high deductible health plans (HDHP) is limited to $6,450 for individuals and $12,900 for families.

Rescissions:

Plans are prohibited from having rescissions or retroactive cancellations or discontinuation of coverage except in cases of fraud or intentional misrepresentation.

Waiting periods:

Group health plans and insurance issuers are prohibited from applying a waiting period for individuals otherwise eligible to enroll that exceeds 90 days. The 90 days is comprised of all calendar days including weekends and holidays. Being “otherwise eligible to enroll” under the terms of the plan means the individual has satisfied the plan's substantive eligibility terms.

Orientation Periods:

Plans are permitted to use an “orientation period” in addition to the 90-day waiting period as long as:

  • The period is not more than one month; and
  • The maximum 90-day waiting period begins on the first day after the orientation period

Excise Tax or “Cadillac Tax” on High-Cost Plans:

Beginning in 2020, if an employer offers a high-cost plan, an insurer, plan administrator or employer will be subject to an excise tax of 40% of the value of the health insurance benefits exceeding the threshold. If the total cost of a group health plan exceeds certain thresholds (the anticipated amount in 2020 is $10,200 for individual coverage and $27,500 for family coverage), the plan is considered a “high-cost” or a “Cadillac” plan.

The cost of a plan is determined by the total premiums paid by the employer and the employee as well as the contributions made by both parties to plans such as Flexible Spending Accounts, Health Reimbursement Accounts, Health Savings Accounts and Employee Assistance Plans. The cost of vision and dental plans will not be factored when determining an employer's plan cost for purposes of the Cadillac Tax.

Employers should speak to their brokers and insurers well in advance of 2020 to discuss the cost of coverage and ways to help avoid this tax.

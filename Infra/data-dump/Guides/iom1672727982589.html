Pregnancy-Related Leave

Whether you are required to provide leave for pregnancy-related conditions depends on your size, location, and how you treat other employees similar in their ability or inability to work.

FMLA:

The FMLA requires covered employers to provide female employees with unpaid, job-protected leave for incapacity due to pregnancy, prenatal care, or their own serious health condition following the birth of their child. Eligible employees (mothers or fathers) may also use all or part of their FMLA leave entitlement to bond with their baby after childbirth or placement or for a child's serious health condition.

State requirements:

Several states have family leave laws that require employers to provide pregnancy-related leave or to provide a reasonable accommodation for pregnant employees, which may include a leave of absence. Check your state law to ensure compliance.

Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC):

The EEOC states that an employer is generally required to treat a pregnant worker the same as other employees who are similar in their ability or inability to work. This means that if an employer provides leave for other temporarily disabled employees, it may be required to provide leave to pregnant employees as well.

Americans with Disabilities Act:

While a typical pregnancy won't qualify as a disability under the ADA, complications during or after pregnancy or childbirth may result in a disability requiring a reasonable accommodation, such as a leave of absence. Generally, employers are required to provide a reasonable accommodation, unless it would impose an undue hardship on the business. Engage in the interactive process (have a conversation) in a timely manner with the employee to determine if a leave of absence, or other accommodation is necessary.

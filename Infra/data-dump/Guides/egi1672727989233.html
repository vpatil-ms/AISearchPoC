Here are answers to some frequently asked questions about disciplining employees.

Q: Do we have to give poor performers an opportunity to improve? If so, how much time should we give them?

A: It is a best practice to monitor employee performance closely, provide frequent feedback, and intervene promptly when a performance issue arises. Generally, you should give poor performers a reasonable opportunity to improve. Depending on the circumstances, employers typically put employees on a 30-, 60-, or 90-day performance improvement plan.

Q: During a meeting about performance, an employee revealed that they have a disability that is affecting their work. Do I have to take this into consideration when evaluating their performance?

A: The employee's performance should be evaluated the same way regardless of whether the employee has a disability. However, when an employer learns of the employee's disability (the employee may use other terms such as “medical condition”), the employer should begin a discussion with the employee to determine whether the condition qualifies as a disability, to what extent it impacts the employee's performance, and if a reasonable accommodation could address the problem under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

Note: The ADA applies to employers with 15 or more employees, but many states have similar laws that apply to smaller employers.

Q: During a disciplinary meeting, an employee informed me that a mental illness caused their misconduct. Can the employee still be disciplined?

A: If an employee violates a conduct rule that is job-related and consistent with business necessity, and all employees are held to the same standard, an employer may discipline the employee for their misconduct under the ADA. Examples of workplace rules that are generally considered job-related and consistent with business necessity include, but are not limited to, rules prohibiting violence, threats of violence, stealing, or destruction of property. Consider consulting legal counsel if you have specific questions regarding disciplinary action for misconduct by an employee with a mental or physical disability.

Q What can I do if an employee refuses to sign a written warning?

A: Explain that the purpose of the signature is to confirm that the employee received the information, not that they necessarily agree with it. Give the employee an opportunity to provide a written statement if they disagree with the warning. If the employee still refuses to sign, note that on the written warning and then date it.

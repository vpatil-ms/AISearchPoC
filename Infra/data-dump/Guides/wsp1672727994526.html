The child labor provisions of the FLSA place restrictions on the hours and types of work minors can perform. Some states also have child labor laws. Check your state law to ensure compliance.

Hazardous Work:

The FLSA sets the minimum age for jobs that are deemed “particularly hazardous” at 18. However, in agriculture, the minimum age for hazardous work is 16.

Agriculture (under age 16):

In the agricultural industry, some of the hazardous duties minors under the age of 16 are prohibited from performing include, but are not limited to: operating a tractor, trenchers, fork lifts, and power-driven saws; working with dangerous or vulnerable animals; working from a ladder or scaffold over 20 feet high; and driving.

Other industries (under 18):

These 17 Hazardous Orders pertain to non-agricultural industries and prohibit minors under the age of 18 from performing work that involves:

Manufacturing or storing explosives

Driving a motor vehicle and being an outside helper on a motor vehicle

Coal mining

Forest fire fighting and forest fire prevention, timber tract, forestry service, and occupations in logging and sawmilling operations

Operating power-driven woodworking machines

Exposure to radioactive substances and to ionizing radiations

Operating power-driven hoisting equipment

Operating power-driven metal-forming, punching, and shearing machines

Mining, other than coal mining

Operating power-driven meat-processing machines, and slaughtering, meat packing or processing

Operating power-driven bakery machines

Operating balers, compactors, and power driven paper-products machines

Manufacturing brick, tile, and related products

Operating power-driven circular saws, band saws, guillotine shears, chain saws, reciprocating saws, wood chippers, and abrasive cutting discs

Wrecking, demolition, and shipbreaking operations

Working in roofing operations

Working in certain excavation operations

Note: There are some exceptions to these Hazardous Orders in the case of apprentices or student “learners.”

For more information, visit the DOL website.

Non-Hazardous Work:

The minimum age for non-hazardous work is 16 years old (14 years old for agricultural work).

Exceptions (Agricultural):

There are three exceptions to the standard minimum age of 14 in agricultural work, including when the parent consents to the work, the minor is employed by the parent's farm, or works as a hand-harvest laborer for no more than eight weeks in a year.

Exceptions (Non-Agricultural):

For non-agricultural work, a minor 14 or 15 years of age can work in limited, specified jobs:

  • Clerical work
  • Intellectual, artistic, or creative work
  • Cooking with electric or gas grills and other work involving preparing and serving food which does not involve cooking over an open flame
  • Cashiering, selling, modeling, art work, work in advertising departments, window trimming, and comparative shopping
  • Price marking and tagging by hand or machine, assembling orders, packing, and shelving
  • Bagging and carrying out customers' orders
  • Errand and delivery work by foot, bicycle, and public transportation
  • Clean up work, including the use of vacuum cleaners and floor waxers, and the maintenance of grounds, but not including the use of power-driven equipment
  • Cleaning vegetables and fruits, and the wrapping, sealing, labeling, weighing, pricing, and stocking of items when performed in areas physically separate from a freezer or meat cooler.
  • 15 year-old workers may perform lifeguard duties at traditional swimming pools and water amusement parks (excluding the elevated areas of power-driven water slides) upon receiving the proper training and certification

In addition, the following minors are not subject to the FLSA's child labor protections:

Child actors and performers

Children engaged in the delivery of newspapers

Children engaged in babysitting

Hours of Employment:

The FLSA has standardized certain time limits for child labor based on age and industry.

Agricultural:

In agriculture, the only restriction on working hours for children is the prohibition of employment during school hours.

Non-agricultural:

Outside of agriculture, 14- and 15-year-olds cannot work:

  • Before 7:00 a.m., or after 7:00 p.m. when school is in session (after 9:00 p.m. from June 1 through Labor Day due to time off from school).
  • More than 3 hours on a school day (including Fridays)
  • Over 18 hours in a school week
  • Over 8 hours on a non-school day
  • More than 40 hours in a non-school week.

Youth Minimum Wage:

A minimum wage of $4.25 an hour is permitted for employees less than 20 years of age during their first 90 consecutive calendar days of employment. Employers are prohibited from taking any action to displace employees in order to hire employees at the youth minimum wage.

Proof of Age:

Many states require employers to confirm the minor is of legal age to work before hire. The DOL suggests that employers obtain a federal certificate of age issued by the Wage and Hour Division or a state certificate of age issued by the applicable state agency. Many states also consider a certified birth certificate to be an acceptable proof of age.

The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA), which took effect November 2009, protects Americans against discrimination based on their genetic information when it comes to health insurance coverage and employment. The Act prohibits insurers from denying health insurance or setting rates based on genetic factors, and similarly, forbids employers from using such information when making employment decisions. GINA applies to employers with 15 or more employees.

“Genetic information” defined:

“Genetic information” is defined as: (1) information about an individual's genetic tests; (2) information about the genetic tests of a family member;(3) the manifestation of disease or disorder in family members of the individual (family medical history); (4) requests for, and receipt of, genetic services by an individual or a family member; and (5) genetic information about a fetus carried by an individual or family member, or about an embryo legally held by the individual or family member using assisted reproductive technology.

GINA defines “genetic test” as the analysis of human DNA, RNA, chromosomes, proteins, or metabolites that detects genotypes, mutations, or chromosomal changes. Examples of genetic tests, include but are not limited to, the following: tests to determine whether an individual carries the genetic variant evidencing a predisposition to cancer; carrier screening to determine the risk of conditions, such as cystic fibrosis, sickle cell anemia, and spinal muscular atrophy; evaluations used to determine the presence of genetic abnormalities in a fetus; paternity tests; and DNA testing that determines the presence of genetic markers associated with ancestry.

Anti-discrimination provision:

Employers are prohibited from using genetic information when making employment decisions, even if the information was obtained in a lawful manner. The law also prevents employers from retaliating against employees who oppose employment practices that violate GINA or who participate in an investigation under the Act. Furthermore, while not explicitly included in the law, the EEOC considers harassment based on genetic information unlawful.

GINA also has a health insurance component which makes it illegal for health insurers to raise premiums or deny coverage based on genetic information. Chiefly, this provision affects employers who self-insure.

Apart from six narrow exceptions, employers are prohibited from requesting, requiring, or obtaining the genetic information of an applicant or employee, or a family member of an applicant or employee. These exceptions are: (1) where the information is acquired inadvertently (e.g., information that is volunteered by the employee/applicant, overheard, or otherwise received accidentally); (2) information acquired through a voluntary wellness program that provides genetic testing or counseling, so long as such information is not individually identifiable; (3) information provided to the employer in connection with the certification requirements for a family and medical leave request; (4) when the information comes from sources that are commercially and publicly available, such as newspapers, books, magazines, and electronic sources; (5) as part of genetic monitoring that is either required by law or provided on a voluntary basis; and (6) by employers who conduct DNA testing for law enforcement purposes as a forensic lab, or for human remains identification. Employers should review the regulations closely before determining whether an exception applies.

Wellness programs:

Under certain circumstances, employers may acquire genetic information through wellness programs without violating GINA. First, the employee or family member must give prior, voluntary, and written authorization. In addition, no genetic information may be provided to the employer unless it is in aggregate form, so that no specific individual may be identified. Access to individually identifiable genetic information is reserved for the individual (or family member who is receiving genetic services) and the healthcare professionals or genetic counselors providing such services. Furthermore, employers are permitted to offer a financial incentive for individuals to complete a health-risk assessment; however, the incentive may not be contingent upon whether the participant provides genetic information.

Medical certifications:

Employers should review their medical examination forms as well as other forms that request health-related information, such as workers' compensation forms and fitness for duty forms, to ensure that they warn the employee and/or healthcare provider that genetic information mustn't be provided. In GINA regulations, the EEOC included model language for lawfully requesting medical information. Employers are urged to adopt this model language on all applicable forms.

Privacy & confidentiality:

Genetic information is considered “protected health information” for HIPAA purposes and should be treated accordingly. Such information may not be released to any other party, except for a few very limited circumstances. These circumstances include:

  • The person to whom the information relates;
  • An occupational health researcher if the research is conducted under applicable federal regulations;
  • In response to a court order that specifically calls for the information;
  • Government officials investigating compliance with GINA;
  • Disclosure in connection with a request for leave under federal, state or local law; and
  • Disclosure to officials in connection with a contagious disease or imminent life-threatening hazard or illness.

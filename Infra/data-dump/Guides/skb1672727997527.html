Wellness Plans and Incentives

The ACA established a grant program for employers with 100 or fewer employees that offer wellness programs. These employers may be eligible for grants if they did not have a wellness program in place on March 23, 2010.

Categories of Wellness Programs:

Employment-based wellness programs can be divided into two broad categories: participatory wellness programs and health-contingent wellness programs.

Participatory Wellness Programs:

Participatory wellness programs do not require an employee to meet a specific health-related standard to obtain a reward offered by the employer. Some examples of these types of programs include reimbursement for the cost of a fitness center membership or reimbursement for a smoking cessation program. As long as these types of programs are uniformly applied to all similarly situated employees, they comply with the nondiscrimination requirements without having to satisfy additional criteria.

Health-contingent Wellness Programs:

Health-contingent wellness programs require an employee to meet a certain health-related standard or outcome in order to obtain a reward. Examples of these types of programs include achieving specific results due to exercise and diet programs or achieving a specified cholesterol level. These programs must follow certain nondiscrimination rules.

Reasonable Design:

Wellness programs must be reasonably designed. Generally, a wellness program is reasonably designed if it has a reasonable chance of improving the health or preventing disease in participating employees. The program should also be designed so that it is not overly burdensome to the employee, is not a deception for discrimination based on a health factor, and is not highly suspicious in the method chosen to promote health.

Required Components:

To be eligible for the grant, the wellness program must contain the following elements:

  • Health awareness initiatives, including health education, preventive screenings, and health risk assessments;
  • Efforts to maximize employee engagement, including mechanisms to encourage employee participation;
  • Initiatives to change unhealthy behaviors and lifestyle choices, including counseling, seminars, online programs, and self-help materials; and
  • Supportive environments, including workplace policies to encourage healthy lifestyles, healthy eating, increased physical activity, and improved mental health.

Once program funding becomes available, eligible employers may submit a grant application to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). Additional guidance is expected from the HHS regarding the application process and the necessary funding for such a program.

Cost-Control Recommendations

Employers are able to limit the costs of WC. Some of the following may be done in-house while others are legislative or administrative changes to the WC sys­tem:

  • Claims Management: To prevent abuse of WC claims, employers should promptly report every WC claim to their insurance carrier, whether or not the validity of the claim is in question. The best approach to managing claims is to set up a systematic procedure for forwarding claims, delegating the responsibility to dependable individuals at both company head­quarters and branch offices. The employer should consider establish­ing a medical care management program for treatment of compens­able injuries and illnesses.
  • Managed Care Programs: Some states allow employers to establish medical care manage­ment pro­grams for treatment of compens­able injuries and illnesses. Managed care in WC means the employer is affiliated with an organization for which health care providers work (usually an HMO) or that offers a list of physi­cians and other care providers (a PPO). This allows many of the managed-care techniques used to curb non-occupational group health costs to be applied to WC. Typically, employers are required to submit plans to their state's WC Commissioner for approval. Then, employers must seek medical care from providers listed in the plan. Employers with approved plans are usually required to post a notice to employees describing the plan and other related employee rights.
  • Accountability: Establish a budget system that holds managers accountable for WC costs.

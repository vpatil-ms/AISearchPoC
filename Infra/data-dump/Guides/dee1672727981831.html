Form I-9: Acceptable Documents

To complete Section 2 of the I-9 Form, the employee must present either: one document from List A; or one document from List B and one document from List C.

IMPORTANT: You are not permitted to request or require employees to present specific documents for purposes of completing the I-9 Form. Employees are free to present any combination of documents that are considered acceptable when establishing identity and employment authorization. A list of acceptable documents can be found on the last page of the I-9 Form.

List A Documents:

List A documents establish both identity and employment authorization. Therefore, if an employee presents a valid document from List A, you may not ask for any further documentation for I-9 purposes. Acceptable List A documents include:

  • A U.S. Passport or Passport Card. The Passport Card is a wallet size document issued by the U.S. Department of State, and for I-9 purposes, is considered a “passport.”
  • A Permanent Resident Card or Alien Registration Receipt Card. This is also known as a “green card” or Form I-551. A valid Permanent Resident Card verifies that a person is lawfully admitted to the U.S. for permanent residence.
  • A foreign passport that contains a temporary I-551 stamp or temporary I-551 notation on a machine-readable immigrant visa.
  • An employment authorization document that contains a photograph (also known as Form I-766).
  • A foreign passport with Form I-94 or I-94A bearing the same name as the passport and including the alien's nonimmigrant status. This is an acceptable List A document in the case of a nonimmigrant alien authorized to work for a specific employer.
  • Passport from the Federated States of Micronesia (FSM) or the Republic of the Marshall Islands (RMI) with Form I-94 indicating nonimmigrant admission under the Compact of Free Association Between the United States and the FSM or RMI.

List B Documents:

For I-9 purposes, List B documents establish identity only. If an employee presents a List B document, they must also present one document from List C. Acceptable List B documents include:

  • Driver's license or ID card issued by a state or outlying possession of the United States, provided it contains a photograph or information such as name, date of birth, gender, height, eye color, and address
  • ID card issued by federal, state, or local government agencies or entities, provided it contains a photograph or information such as name, date of birth, gender, height, eye color, and address
  • School ID card with a photograph
  • Voter's registration card
  • U.S. military card or draft record
  • Military dependent's ID card
  • U.S. Coast Guard Merchant Mariner Card
  • Native American tribal document
  • Driver's license issued by a Canadian government authority

If an employee is under the age of 18 and unable to present any of these documents, they may present a:

  • School record or report card;
  • Clinic, doctor or hospital record; or
  • Daycare or nursery school record

Note: If you participate in E-Verify, you may only accept List B documents that bear a photograph.

List C Documents:

For the purposes of the I-9, List C documents establish employment authorization only. If an employee presents a List C document, they must also present one document from List B. Acceptable List C documents include:

  • A Social Security Account Number card, unless the card includes one of the following restrictions:

    NOT VALID FOR EMPLOYMENT

    VALID FOR WORK ONLY WITH INS AUTHORIZATION

    VALID FOR WORK ONLY WITH DHS AUTHORIZATION

  • Certification of report of birth issued by the U.S. Department of State (Forms DS-1350, FS-545, FS-240)
  • Original or certified copy of a birth certificate issued by a state, county, municipal authority or outlying territory of the United States bearing an official seal
  • Native American tribal document
  • Form I-197, U.S. Citizen Identification Card
  • Form I-179, Identification Card for Use of Resident Citizen in the United States
  • Employment authorization document issued by the Department of Homeland Security (this doesn't include Form I-766, Employment Authorization Document, from List A.)

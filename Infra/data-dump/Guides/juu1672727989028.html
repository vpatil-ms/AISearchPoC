Exempted Plans

Exempted plans are exempt from certain provisions of the ACA. To maintain exempted status, a plan must meet the following criteria:

  1. The plan was in existence on March 23, 2010, the date the ACA was enacted; and
  2. The plan has not made any “significant changes” since March 23, 2010.

Some of the provisions of the ACA in which exempted plans are exempt include, but are not limited to:

  • The requirement to cover essential benefits
  • 100% preventive care coverage
  • The requirement to allow parents to choose participating pediatricians as primary care providers for their children
  • The requirement to permit women to see OB-GYNs without a referral
  • Providing emergency services that are the same in and out of network
  • A prohibition against having eligibility rules that discriminate in favor of highly compensated employees
  • A requirement to establish an appeals process for coverage determinations and claims
  • Rules on cost sharing and related out-of-pocket maximums
  • Coverage for individuals who participate in clinical trials

“Significant Changes”:

The ACA allows plans to make routine changes without losing exempted status. However, plans will lose their status if “significant changes” are made. These changes include:

  • Significantly cutting or reducing benefits. For example, if a plan decides to no longer cover care for people with diabetes, cystic fibrosis, or HIV/AIDS.
  • Raising co-insurance charges. Typically, co-insurance requires a patient to pay a fixed percentage of a charge (for example, 20% of a hospital bill). Exempted plans cannot increase this percentage.
  • Significantly raising co-payment charges. Compared with the copayments in effect on March 23, 2010, exempted plans will be able to increase those co-pays by no more than the greater of $5 (adjusted annually for medical inflation) or a percentage equal to medical inflation plus 15 percentage points. As an example, under this requirement, if a plan raises its copayment from $30 to $50 over the next 2 years, it will lose its status.
  • Significantly raising deductibles. Compared with the deductible required as of March 23, 2010, exempted plans can only increase deductibles by a percentage equal to medical inflation plus 15 percentage points.
  • Significantly lowering employer contributions. Exempted plans cannot decrease the percent of premiums the employer pays by more than 5 percentage points. For example, these plans are not permitted to decrease their own share and increase the employees' share of the premium from 15% to 25%.
  • Adding or tightening an annual limit. To retain their status as exempted, plans cannot tighten any annual dollar limit in place as of March 23, 2010. Moreover, plans that do not have an annual dollar limit cannot add a new one unless they are replacing a lifetime dollar limit with an annual dollar limit that is at least as high as the lifetime limit (which is more protective of high-cost enrollees).

Recordkeeping Requirements:

Exempted plans must include a statement in their plan documents stating that the health plan is exempted. They must also maintain records documenting that the coverage was established prior to March 23, 2010. These records must be made available upon request.

Once an employer learns about an allegation of sexual harassment, whether the complaint is formal or informal, there is an obligation to investigate. The goal of the investigation is to gather the facts of the case and to determine whether sexual harassment has occurred. If the investigation reveals that harassment has in fact occurred, employers must take action to end the harassment and prevent its reoccurrence.

Because of the importance and sensitive nature of these types of investigations, the process should be handled with extreme care and legal counsel should be consulted as needed. The following are guidelines for conducting an effective sexual harassment investigation:

  • Investigate promptly. The investigation should take place as soon as possible after discovering that harassment may have occurred. When possible, more than one individual should be involved in the investigation, including acting as a witness during interviews. Investigators should be instructed to remain objective and they must be trained on the investigative process.
  • Create a file. To begin, create a confidential file to document the complaint as well as the steps you take throughout the investigation process. This file should be kept in a secure location separate from personnel files.
  • Take all complaints seriously. It's important that employees feel comfortable reporting any instance in which they believe harassment has occurred. This can be undermined if employees believe their complaints won't be treated with the proper attention. As such, employers must take every complaint seriously and avoid any retaliatory action against complainants.
  • Prepare to interview appropriate parties. Prior to beginning the investigation, determine who will be interviewed and prepare a list of questions. Arrange to conduct the interviews in a private location and plan on obtaining signed written statements from each interviewee attesting to the information provided during the interview.
  • Interview the accuser. Those conducting the investigation should explain the investigation process, including their role as a neutral fact-finder. They should ask the complainant questions related to what happened, who was involved, when it happened, and how it has affected him or her. Other questions should address whether there were any witnesses or whether any other co-workers may have been subjected to the same or similar behavior. Take detailed notes and repeat them back to the complainant to ensure your understanding of the incident.
  • Interview witnesses. Start by informing the witness that you are investigating possible misconduct and ask if they have witnessed or have been subjected to any inappropriate conduct. If they have information pertaining to the alleged incident, ask for the specifics of what they observed before, during, and after. Avoid unnecessarily disclosing information pertaining to the investigation and stress the importance of cooperating fully as well as your commitment to maintaining confidentiality.
  • Interview the accused. Explain the accusations that have been made against the individual. Describe the investigation process and assure him or her that you are a neutral fact-finder and that no decision has yet been made with regard to the merits of the complaint. Ask the individual for his or her version of events and whether there are any witnesses or evidence that may corroborate it. Ensure that the individual provides a response to each of the complainant's allegations and advise the accused that retaliation against the complainant or any witnesses is forbidden.
  • Gather evidence. If there is any evidence of the harassment, such as emails, instant messages, or video surveillance, it's important to take all necessary steps to protect it. Implement appropriate security measures to ensure that evidence isn't destroyed or tampered with.
  • Evaluate credibility. Without a “smoking gun,” the resolution of sexual harassment complaints often depends on the credibility of those involved in the investigation. Some things to consider when determining one's credibility include the consistency of the statements made, demeanor during the interviews, any corroborative or contradictory evidence as well as the individual's behavior immediately following the alleged incident.
  • Prepare the report. When preparing the report, include a chronology of events, a list of witnesses, the facts of the case, any evidence you may have been able to obtain, and the conclusions you have made regarding the incident(s) in question. You may also want to include recommendations for preventing this type of behavior in the future and if you made a finding of harassment, any corrective action that will be taken against the responsible individual.
  • Take corrective action. If the investigation reveals that sexual harassment occurred, take immediate and appropriate corrective action. The corrective measure may include training, job transfer, suspension, termination, or any other disciplinary action appropriate based on the severity of the conduct. When administering any type of corrective action, it's important to be consistent with how you have handled similar situations in the past.
  • Follow-up. After you remediate the issue, it's important to check in with the victim occasionally to ensure the harassment has not resumed. If necessary, take further corrective action and update the written report with any new developments.

Effective investigation procedures can not only help to uncover inappropriate conduct, but they can also serve to document your good faith efforts to respond to accusations of wrongdoing. When done with the necessary care, these investigations can be a powerful tool for preventing sexual harassment and maintaining a safe and productive work environment. Remember that all claims of sexual harassment should be taken seriously and you should consult with legal counsel as needed.

Maintaining an Investigation File:

When conducting an investigation it is important to create a file which includes information such as what actions were taken, who was involved, and all the communications. Here are some examples of things to include:

  • A log of the investigator's actions and calls with dates and times
  • Progressive and final interview notes for each witness
  • All communications to and from witnesses, including the alleged victims and the alleged harasser
  • All draft and final witness statements
  • All complaints
  • All documents (including tape recordings or other physical evidence) that support or refute the allegations of the complaint
  • The investigator's report
  • All documents reflecting notification of the investigation results and any remedial action taken

Remember, any notes of conversations or communications between your legal counsel and the company should be maintained in a separate file. In addition, the investigation file should not contain the investigator's opinions or conclusions, unless they're necessary for the final report or to assess the credibility of a witness.

Sometimes an alleged harasser might wish to have a co-worker represent him or her. If the circumstances allow it, it is recommended to grant the employee's request. This can help give the accused employee a sense of fairness in the investigation. However, if this second individual is a witness or observer to the alleged harassment it is recommended that you see the two individuals separately so that you may focus on one perspective at a time.

How to Conduct a Sexual Harassment Interview:

Interviewing anyone can be an intimidating task. Interviewing victims, alleged harassers and witnesses can be an even more challenging task. The topic is highly sensitive--emotions will run the gamut from nervousness and anger, to fear and feelings of violation. To complicate matters the facts and details of the case are extremely important and vital to obtain. As a result it important to take every measure possible to prepare yourself for these interviews.

It is important to plan out before an interview begins what information you need to obtain from the individual you are interviewing. Also, try to anticipate some of the emotions that they might express. Close your eyes and visualize how you might handle the responses you could get from them. It may sound silly but it can be very helpful to preparing yourself.

Be prepared, thorough and concise in your question asking. Remember to be as objective as possible and do not arrive at any conclusions until you have all the facts. Most importantly remember to stay calm, even the best trained manager would find a situation such as this difficult to handle.

Always conduct each interview separately and as soon as is appropriate upon hearing of the case for the first time. When beginning each interview make sure that the employee feels comfortable with the investigator and the location of the interview.

Explain to both the alleged victim and harasser the entire process that is being conducted, including how the final decision will be determined and when. Provide the employees with a copy of the company's sexual harassment policy and confirm the employee's receipt of the policies in the interview notes. In addition to providing a copy of your company's policies, make sure that the employees understand:

  • That the company will not tolerate or condone improper conduct;
  • That the company will conduct a prompt and thorough investigation to determine whether inappropriate conduct has occurred;
  • If the company determines that inappropriate conduct occurred, it will be stopped and appropriate corrective action will be taken;
  • There will be no retaliation against the employee for bringing an honestly-believed complaint; and
  • The employee is expected to provide a thorough and truthful account of what has occurred and to identify all evidence and possible witnesses.
  • Caution: Don't promise the employee total confidentiality. You can, however, confirm that the information will only be shared with those who have a “need to know.”

* Preface this information to the alleged harasser(s) that you are required to communicate this information regardless of whether or not they are innocent or not of the allegations. It is important to communicate this to try and limit defensiveness.

Information that should be obtained through the interviews includes:

A complete list of every act and statement the employee believes to have been instances of sexual harassment, offensive conduct, or that contributed to a hostile work environment. For each instance, be sure the accuser discloses the following:

  • When, where, and what occurred, with as much detail as possible;
  • Who was present;
  • What conversation and/or conduct occurred with the alleged harasser before the incident;
  • How he or she responded to the alleged harasser when the conduct occurred;
  • What he or she said to the alleged harasser the next time they saw each other;
  • Whether the employee ever did or said anything to the alleged harasser that would indicate that he or she had welcomed (or at least not been offended by) his or her behavior;
  • Whether he or she indicated that the act or statement offended him or her and, if so, how and when;
  • Whether he or she asked the alleged harasser to stop;
  • To whom did the employee first report the offensive conduct and whether or not he or she spoke with anyone else about this behavior; if so, ask who, when, and what was discussed;
  • Whether he or she made any notes, record or tape recordings of the conduct (if so, obtain a copy).
  • Whether he or she continued normal activities after the incident, or if he or she sought any medical treatment or counseling as a result.

You'll also want to inquire whether or not the complaining party was aware of your company's sexual harassment policy and complaint procedure and, if so, when he or she first become aware of it. Did he or she promptly use the complaint procedure? If not, find out why. What action does the employee wants the company to take? Does the employee believe he or she can continue to work with the alleged harasser and, if so, what the company can do to help him or her resume a positive working relationship with that individual?

More specifically, some important questions to ask the alleged victim include:

  • What is the name of the harasser?
  • What are the facts of the case?
  • How often did it occur? Is it still ongoing?
  • Where did it occur? What are your perceptions of this behavior?
  • Is there any physical evidence related to the case?
  • Were there other employees present?
  • Might anyone else have been harassed?
  • Have you told anyone else about this behavior?
  • How would you like to see the situation resolved?*
  • Is there any other pertinent information you would like to share with me?

* Sometimes the victim may simply want to be transferred or to have the alleged harasser warned. While you may often be able to grant the employees wish, to protect other employees and your organization it is necessary to stick with your company's stated policy for sexual harassment. For example, simply transferring the employee may not stop the behavior of the harasser and as a result, other employees may be at risk. This also increases the company's liability for not appropriately responding to such behaviors.

Some important questions to ask the alleged harasser include:

  • What are the facts of the case?
  • What is your response to the grievances?

Potential follow-up questions:

  • If the other person is falsifying information, why might this be the case?
  • How often did this situation occur? Is it still ongoing?
  • Where did it occur? What are your perceptions of this behavior?
  • Is there any physical evidence related to the case?
  • Were there other employees present or involved?
  • Is there any other pertinent information you would like to share with me?

What to do with your Investigation Results:

Once you have interviewed all employees involved in a sexual harassment case, you have reviewed all pertinent documentation and facts, there are three possible outcomes:

You determine that:

  • Sexual harassment has occurred;
  • Sexual harassment has not occurred and the complaint was unfounded; or
  • Based on your investigation, you cannot reach a decision.

Your actions will depend on the result of the investigation.

Sexual harassment has occurred:

If your investigation reveals that sexual harassment has occurred, you must take appropriate corrective action. The kind of appropriate corrective action will depend on the severity of the misconduct and the particular facts of the case, but it could include:

  • Discharging or reprimanding the harasser;
  • Placing the harasser on suspension or probation;
  • Reinstating the victim with back pay;
  • Transferring or demoting the harasser; and
  • Warning or disciplining all employees who failed to report the harassment.

Once you know that harassment has occurred, you have a legal obligation to prevent it from continuing and to remedy the situation for any other victims of the harasser. You must make any necessary follow-up to resolve the harassment and eliminate any of its lingering effects. If you don't discharge the harasser, you must take affirmative steps to prevent any future sexual harassment complaint.

Sexual harassment has not occurred:

Following your investigation, if you've determined that the allegation is without merit, you should thoroughly document why you believe that the allegation was unfounded and carefully organize your file. If the accuser files a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) or with the courts, you'll have to prove that you met your legal obligations to the employee by conducting an impartial investigation. That's why it's crucial that you have a solid sexual harassment policy and that you maintain a complete paper trail documenting all action taken for each sexual harassment complaint.

The simplest way to protect your company is to focus on the facts, treat the parties impartially, and act in good faith. Your investigative file should reflect on paper your fairness, objectivity, and impartiality.

Because of the hostility that naturally arises from these types of allegations, it's prudent to separate the two employees from working together, if possible.

CAUTION: Be particularly careful about disciplining an employee who makes an unfounded sexual harassment complaint. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits employers from retaliating against an employee for making a sexual harassment complaint, even if it's unfounded. He or she can't be disciplined for making a good faith complaint, even if it's without merit.

Inconclusive investigation:

Either from a lack of evidence or uncooperative witnesses, sexual harassment investigators often can't reach a definitive conclusion of whether or not harassment has occurred. In this case, you should clearly document the investigation and spell out why you cannot come to a conclusion. Again, the employer may consider whether separating the two employees is an appropriate course of action.

Follow up:

After concluding the investigation, be sure to follow up with the alleged victim and the alleged harasser(s). The investigator should let the grieving party know the results of the investigation. Generally, you may tell him or her whether disciplinary action has been taken, but don't share details of the type of disciplinary action with him or her. Be sure to give the grieving party a chance to ask questions. Finally, the investigator should periodically check in with the employee to confirm that the harassment has not begun again and that he or she has not been subjected to retaliation. These “check-ins” should occur in a private place, not in front of other employees.

When you follow up with the alleged harasser, clearly explain the results of the investigation, review once again the company's policies and explain what disciplinary action, if any, is being taken against him or her and why. Give the alleged harasser a chance to ask any questions. Finally, be sure he or she understands any future harassing conduct will not be tolerated and that he or she is not to retaliate against the complaining party in any way.

* Remember to document all steps that are taken in the investigation and in the follow-up process.

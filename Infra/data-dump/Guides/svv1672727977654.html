Interview Tips

Consider these tips for conducting an effective interview:

  • Prepare: Set aside adequate time prior to the interview to review the candidate's application materials. If you have conducted a phone or video interview, review any notes you made during your discussion with the applicant. After reviewing these materials, prepare a list of relevant interview questions.
  • Be consistent: Consistency is important for demonstrating that your interview process is applied fairly. This means all candidates for a particular position should be asked the same core set of questions.
  • Ask only job-related questions: Federal, state, and local laws protect employees and job applicants from discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, disability, genetic information, and military status, among other characteristics. Only ask job-related questions and avoid questions that could, directly or indirectly, identify the candidate as a member of a protected group. If an applicant voluntarily offers information about their protected status, redirect the interview to elicit job-related information that can help assess whether the candidate is qualified for the job and do not use the protected information when making employment decisions. Train supervisors and others who conduct interviews to ask questions in accordance with the law and company policy.
  • Ask open-ended questions: Closed-ended questions can be answered with a simple yes or no and are often leading questions (the appropriate answer can be determined by the question itself). Open-ended questions are more likely to elicit valuable job-related information by encouraging applicants to give a fuller answer based on their own knowledge, experience, and beliefs. Instead of asking a closed-ended question such as “are you adept at handling disputes between employees?” you may want to ask the candidate to describe a situation in which they had to handle a workplace dispute in the past.
  • Consider behavioral based questioning: Behavioral questions ask candidates to share past experiences handling specific work challenges; those they'll likely encounter working for you. When asking behavioral questions, ask the candidate to provide details concerning the situation, the action they took, and the end result. For example, a behavioral-based question for a customer service representative may be “tell me about a time when you received a call from an irate customer and how you handled it.” A question for a managerial candidate may be “describe a situation in which two employees were having a dispute and how you handled it.” Follow-up questions are an important component to behavioral-based interviews. For example, if the candidate repeatedly uses "we," the interviewer should ask about their specific role in addressing the work challenge. Another follow-up question to consider is asking why the candidate chose a particular course of action over others, which can provide insight into their motivations, thought process, and problem-solving ability. Follow-up questions can also help the interviewer determine the authenticity of the information provided by the candidate.
  • Take notes: Jot down notes during the interview so that you can recall valuable information when it comes time to compare candidates and ultimately make a hiring decision. Keep notes factual and avoid expressing opinions or including information that is unrelated to the job, even if voluntarily disclosed by the applicant.
  • Evaluate responses: After the interview, promptly review responses. Evaluate relevance to the question asked and the candidate's effectiveness in handling the work challenge.

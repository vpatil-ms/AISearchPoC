Medical Exams & Certifications:

Generally, the ADA permits employers to conduct pre-hire medical examinations after an offer of employment has been made and after all potentially disqualifying steps of the pre-employment process have been completed, provided that all new employees in the same type of job are subject to the exam. The results must be kept confidential and the exam must be job-related.

If an employee requests an accommodation, employers may ask the employee for reasonable medical documentation only if the disability or the need for an accommodation isn't obvious. In such cases, employers may only ask for information that is necessary to process the accommodation request. This means that an employer may seek information needed to establish that the individual has a disability, and that the disability creates functional limitations that require a reasonable accommodation. However, the employer may not ask for an employee's complete medical file.

Note: Medical records must be kept in confidential files, separate from the employee's personnel file.

Recordkeeping Requirements:

Under the ADA, employers must retain records related to:

  • Hiring (e.g., resumes, application forms and interview notes)
  • Requests for reasonable accommodation
  • Promotion, demotion, transfer, lay-off or termination
  • Rates of pay or other terms of compensation
  • Selection for training

Employers must retain these records for one year after making the record or taking the action (whichever occurs later). If a discrimination complaint is filed or an action is brought by the EEOC, an employer must save all personnel records related to the complaint until its final disposition.

Job Descriptions:

While not required by the ADA, it is a best practice to maintain written job descriptions for each job. Among other things, job descriptions are beneficial when hiring, processing accommodation requests, and making other employment decisions. Generally, job descriptions should include the essential functions of the job, necessary qualifications, and skills. If the physical demands and/or work environment are essential to the job, they should be included in the job description as well. If you include physical demands, focus on the task that needs to be done, rather than how it should be done. For example, say that the position requires “moving” 50 pounds, instead of “lifting” 50 pounds, or “traversing” the length of the warehouse instead of “walking” the length of the warehouse. Employees with disabilities may be able to perform the essential functions of the job with accommodation, such as using a cart, dolly, or mobility aid. Review job descriptions regularly to ensure that they accurately reflect the current requirements of the job.

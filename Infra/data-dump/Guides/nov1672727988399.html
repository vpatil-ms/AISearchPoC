Typically, credit checks are performed on individuals applying for select positions, such as those that call for a great deal of financial responsibility. However, many states and local jurisdictions limit the use of credit checks for employment purposes. Most of these laws require credit checks to be “substantially job-related.”

While each law defines job relatedness differently, most include positions with access to client or company financial data, such as positions that:

  • Are managerial and involve setting direction or control of a business unit;
  • Entail a fiduciary responsibility, such as the authority to issue payments, collect debts, transfer money, or enter into contracts;
  • Are provided an expense account or corporate credit card;
  • Have access to the company's trade secrets, intellectual property, or other confidential business information.

Check your state and local law before conducting a credit check.

EEOC Guidance:

Meanwhile, the EEOC has taken the stance that if the use of credit checks has a disparate impact on a protected group, they must be job-related and consistent with a business necessity (see the Criminal Records section of this guide for more information).

FCRA Requirements:

If you decide to obtain credit reports, you must comply with the Fair Credit Reporting Act (see the Rules for Conducting Background Checks section of this guide). Credit reports are a type of consumer report and, thus, subject to the FCRA.

Bankruptcy Code:

The Bankruptcy Code generally prohibits employment discrimination against an individual who has filed for bankruptcy protection. Do not deny employment to an individual solely on the basis of an applicant's or employee's bankruptcy.

ACA Checklist for Small Businesses

Small employers, those with less than 50 full-time and FTE employees as defined by the ACA, are not subject to the employer mandate. However, small employers are subject to other requirements under the ACA. Use the checklist below to help you understand some of these requirements.

All Employers

Determine “employer size” as it relates to the ACA:

Employers with 50 or more full-time and equivalent employees must offer full-time employees the opportunity to enroll in minimum essential health coverage. To determine employer size under the ACA, use the following steps:

  1. Determine controlled group status: A controlled group is a group of employers treated as a single employer under section 414(b), (c), (m), or (o) of the Internal Revenue Code, and are counted as a single employer to determine whether together they employed 50 or more full-time and FTE employees in the prior calendar year.
  2. Calculate number of full-time employees: Determine the number of employees who work an average of at least 30 hours per week.
  3. Calculate number of FTEs: Add all the hours worked by your part-time employees (those working less than 30 hours per week) in a month and divide by 120. Note: If the part-time employee works more than 120 hours but less than 130 hours during the month, only 120 hours must be included in the calculation.
  4. Arrive at a total number of FTEs: Add the number of full-time employees and FTE employees to arrive at your final number. Round down to the nearest whole number.

“Small” employers have less than 50 full-time and FTE employees and are not subject to the employer mandate. Arriving at this calculation can be complicated. Consider working with your tax advisor, insurance carrier and/or legal counsel.

Notify New Hires of Health Insurance Marketplace:

Employers must notify all new hires, regardless of their plan enrollment status or whether they are part-time or full-time, of the availability of the Health Insurance Marketplace within 14 days of hire.

There are two Model Notices of Coverage Options: one for employers offering health coverage and one for employers not offering coverage.

For Employers that Offer Health Insurance

Is your health plan considered “grandfathered”?

Grandfathered plans are exempt from certain provisions of the ACA (see Grandfathered Plans for more information). To determine if you maintain grandfathered status, ask yourself:

  1. Was your plan in existence on March 23, 2010?
  2. Have you refrained from making “significant changes” to the plan? Significant changes include but are not limited to, significantly cutting or reducing benefits, raising co-insurance charges, significantly raising co-payment charges, significantly raising deductibles, significantly lowering employer contributions, and adding or tightening an annual limit.

If you answered “yes” to both questions, your plan may be considered grandfathered. Plans that wish to maintain grandfathered status must include in plan materials a statement describing the benefits provided under the plan, that the plan believes it is a grandfathered health plan, and contact information for questions or complaints.

Does your plan offer “Essential Health Benefits”?

The ACA requires that certain non-grandfathered group health plans offer a standard package of benefits referred to as “essential health benefits” (see Major Insurance Reforms for more information). Review plan documents and work with your insurance carrier to ensure that essential health benefits are provided for all health plans.

Does your plan offer “Preventive Services”?

Under the ACA, all non-grandfathered health plans must cover “recommended” preventive services and “routine” immunizations without subjecting participants to cost-sharing measures when such services are delivered by an in-network provider (see Major Insurance Reforms for more information).

  • Review plan documents and work with your insurance carrier to ensure that “recommended” preventive services and “routine” immunizations are covered in your health insurance plan.
  • Ensure these services are provided without subjecting participants to cost-sharing measures, such as a co-pay, deductible, or co-insurance, when such services are delivered by an in-network provider.

Consider Other Health Insurance Reforms:

Employers that offer group health coverage should be familiar with additional insurance reforms under the ACA (see Major Insurance Reforms for more information). Work with your insurance carrier to ensure that your plan:

  • Prohibits annual dollar limits
  • Has removed pre-existing conditions exclusions
  • Covers dependents up to the age of 26
  • Restricts waiting periods to a maximum of 90 calendar days

This is not a full list of all insurance reforms under the ACA. To help ensure compliance, carefully evaluate your plan and work with your insurer on plan design.

Incentives for Small Employers

Consider Implementing a Wellness Program:

The ACA has established a grant program for employers with fewer than 100 employees that offer wellness programs. Note: Only non-grandfathered health plans are eligible. To help assess eligibility, ask yourself the following questions:

  • Do you have less than 100 employees?
  • Did you have a wellness program in place on March 23, 2010? Employers may be eligible for grants only if they did not have a wellness program in place on March 23, 2010.
  • Does your wellness program meet the “reasonable design” rule? Generally, a wellness program is reasonably designed if it has a reasonable chance of improving the health or preventing disease in participating employees.
  • Does your wellness program include the necessary elements? The program must include health awareness initiatives, efforts to maximize employee engagement, initiatives to change unhealthy behaviors and lifestyle choices, and supportive environments to encourage healthy lifestyles.

See Wellness Plans and Incentives for more information.

Recommendation:

Employers should work closely with their insurance carrier and legal counsel to help ensure their health plan and workplace policies and procedures comply with the various ACA requirements.

Your Sexual Harassment Policy

Preventing sexual harassment in your organization is best achieved by establishing a zero-tolerance policy. Most importantly, your policy should state your position on harassment and intent to take appropriate dis­ciplinary action against violators. It should be included in your employee handbook and posted on all bulletin boards. Include the following points:

  • Coverage: Specify the groups that are protected from harassment by state and federal law, but also indi­cate that harassment of any individual will not be permitted. Note that temporary employees and contractors are also protected.
  • Definition: Specify what types of acts constitute harassment; point out that employees must treat others respectfully.
  • Complaint Procedures: Your policy should indicate the individual to whom employees should report alleged harassment. This is usually the supervisor, or the human resources department. If employees are to complain to a supervisor, indicate the person to whom employees should complain if the alleged harassment has been committed by a supervisor. Be certain that employees on all shifts and in all locations have a complaint system.
  • Supervisors' Responsibilities: Inform supervisors to whom they should report complaints and what actions they should take upon receiving a complaint. Protocol for handling general harassment may be part of sexual harassment training.
  • Indicate who will conduct the investigation of the alleged harassment. Your policy should provide that instances of harassment will be investigated even if no formal complaint is filed. State that all employees are expected to cooperate with an investigation and that giving false information in the course of an investigation is grounds for discipline.
  • Disciplinary Action: List the types of action that may be taken in response to policy violations, depending on the severity of the harassment and other factors.
  • Privacy: Disclosure of information about complaints should be limited to those with a legitimate involvement. Your policy should state that information about complaints will be held in confidence to the extent possible but do not guarantee absolute confidentiality.
  • Prevention: Explain management's responsibility to notify workers of the harassment policy and its enforcement.
  • Retaliation: Employees should know that they are protected from retaliation if they file a complaint or participate in an investigation.

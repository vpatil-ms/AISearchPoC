The FLSA provides for certain exemptions from the minimum wage and/or overtime pay provisions. Keep in mind that these exemptions are narrowly defined and very few employees will meet the criteria necessary to be considered exempt.

Employees exempt from both overtime and minimum wage:

  • Certain executive, administrative, and professional employees, outside sales employees, and employees in certain computer-related occupations. These employees must meet very specific requirements to be classified as exempt (see Tests for Determining Exempt Status).
  • Employees of certain seasonal amusement or recreational establishments, employees of certain small newspapers, seamen employed on foreign vessels, employees engaged in fishing operations, and employees engaged in newspaper delivery.
  • Farmworkers employed by employers who used no more than 500 “man-days” of farm labor in any calendar quarter of the preceding calendar year. Man-days are calculated by multiplying the number of farmworkers by the number of days they worked in the calendar quarter.
  • Casual babysitters and persons employed as companions to the elderly or infirm.

Employees exempt from overtime, but not minimum wage:

  • Certain commissioned employees of retail or service establishments; sales-workers, parts-clerks, or mechanics servicing auto, truck, trailer, farm implement, boat, or aircrafts who are employed by non-manufacturing establishments.
  • Employees of railroads and air carriers, taxi drivers, certain employees of motor carriers, seamen on American vessels, and local delivery employees paid on approved trip rate plans.
  • Announcers, news editors, and chief engineers of certain non-metropolitan broadcasting stations.
  • Domestic service workers living in the employer's residence.
  • Movie theater employees.
  • Farmworkers that work for an employer that used more than 500 “man-days” of farm labor in any calendar quarter of the preceding calendar year.

Some workers qualify for partial exemptions from the FLSA overtime requirements. These include:

  • Certain employees of amusement and recreational establishments located in national parks and similar facilities if paid overtime for hours after 56 hours in a workweek
  • Bulk or wholesale petroleum distributors if paid overtime for hours after 56 hours in a workweek
  • Employees receiving literacy training for 10 hours per workweek
  • Hospital and nursing home employees if paid overtime after 8 hours per day or 80 hours during two-week periods

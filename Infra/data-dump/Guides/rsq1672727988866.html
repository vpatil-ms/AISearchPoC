Many employers contribute to some type of retirement plan for their employees. Aside from their utility as an element of compensation and as a means of forced savings, such plans are a good way of sheltering otherwise taxable income. For that reason, these plans are of particular value to highly compensated employees.

Common types of company-sponsored retirement plans:

401(k) Plans:

An employee's 401(k) contributions are automatically deducted from his or her paycheck each pay period. Traditional 401(k) plans allow employees to make contributions on a pre-tax basis. In a Roth 401(k) plan employee contributions are taxed, but eligible distributions from the account (including earnings) are generally tax-free. With both types of plans, contributions are invested at the employee's direction into one or more funds provided in the plan. Employers often “match” employee contributions up to a certain percentage, but are not required to do so.

Simplified Employee Pension (SEP) Plans:

A Simplified Employee Pension (SEP) plan provides business owners with a simplified method to contribute toward their employees' retirement as well as their own retirement savings. Contributions are made to an Individual Retirement Account or Annuity (IRA) set up for each plan participant (a SEP-IRA). An SEP does not have the start-up and operating costs of a conventional retirement plan.

Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees (SIMPLE) IRA:

A Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees (SIMPLE) IRA plan provides small employers with a simplified method to contribute toward their employees' and their own retirement savings. In general, employers must have 100 or fewer employees and no other retirement plan to establish a SIMPLE IRA. Employees may choose to make salary reduction contributions and the employer is required to make either matching or non-elective contributions. Contributions are made to an Individual Retirement Account or Annuity (IRA) set up for each employee (a SIMPLE IRA).

OSHA Approved State Plans

Each U.S. state has the right to develop their own standards under a federally approved state plan. The standards under a state plan may differ from federal OSHA regulations, but must provide for at least as much protection as the federal standards. Hence, states have the option to promulgate standards covering hazards not addressed by federal standards; however, most states adopt standards identical to the federal requirements. Click here for a list of OSHA-approved state plans.

Businesses operating in states with their own OSHA approved state plans must comply with their states' standards. If a business is operating in a state that does not have their own OSHA approved plan, then the company must comply with federal OSHA regulations.

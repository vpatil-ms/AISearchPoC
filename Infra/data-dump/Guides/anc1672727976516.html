Overview

In March 2010, President Obama signed the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (Pub. L. No. 111-148) and the Health Care and Education Affordability Reconciliation Act (Pub. L. No. 111-152) into law. Collectively, the law is referred to as the “ACA” or “Health Care Reform.” The ACA has implications for insurance companies, employers, and individuals.

Key Provisions:

Some of the key provisions of the ACA are listed below. However, employers should be aware that there may be additional aspects of the law that impact their business.

  • Essential Health Benefits: Certain plans must cover at least what has been deemed “essential health benefits”
  • Pre-existing Condition Exclusions: Plans are prohibited from denying coverage to individuals with pre-existing conditions
  • Preventive Services: Health plans must cover “recommended” preventive and “routine” immunizations without cost-sharing
  • Dependent Coverage Expanded: Young adults can stay on their parent's plan until the age of 26
  • Annual and Lifetime Limits: Health plans are prohibited from imposing annual and lifetime limits on “essential benefits”
  • Flexible Spending Accounts (FSAs): Employee contributions to health flexible spending arrangements (FSAs) are limited to $2,550 per year
  • Health Insurance Marketplaces: State-based Marketplaces (also known as “exchanges,” or “SHOP”s for small businesses) for individuals and small employers to purchase health coverage
  • Small Business Health Care Tax Credit: Small businesses that offer health coverage under a “qualifying arrangement” may be eligible for a tax credit
  • Break Requirement for Nursing Mothers: Reasonable break time must be provided for an employee to express breast milk for her nursing child
  • Wellness Program Grants: Grants are available for small employers that implement wellness programs and meet certain criteria
  • Cadillac Tax: An excise tax for high-cost health plans
  • Employer Mandate: Also referred to as “shared responsibility,” the employer mandate requires employers with at least 50 full-time and full-time equivalent (FTE) employees to offer health coverage that meets certain requirements or face a possible penalty
  • Individual Mandate: This mandate requires most U.S. citizens and legal residents to have qualifying health coverage or pay a tax penalty

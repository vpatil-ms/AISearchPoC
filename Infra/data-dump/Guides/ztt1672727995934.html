The federal Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), as amended, prohibits:

  • Citizenship or immigration status discrimination;
  • National origin discrimination;
  • Unfair document practices; and
  • Retaliation

In addition, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (“Title VII”) and other federal laws also prohibit employment discrimination on the basis of, among other things, national origin, race, and color.

Citizenship Status & National Origin Discrimination:

Under the INA, citizenship or immigration status discrimination occurs when an employer treats employees differently based on their real or perceived citizenship or immigration status. National origin discrimination occurs when an employer treats employees differently based on their national origin. An employee's national origin relates to the employee's place of birth, country of origin, ethnicity, ancestry, native language, accent, or the perception that they look or sound “foreign.”

Unfair Document Practices:

Document abuse occurs when employers treat individuals differently on the basis of national origin or citizenship status when completing the I-9 Form. Document abuse can be broadly categorized into four types, including improperly:

  • Requesting that employees produce more documents than required;
  • Requesting that employees present a particular document;
  • Rejecting documents that reasonably appear to be genuine and to relate to the employee presenting them; and
  • Treating groups of applicants differently when completing the I-9 Form, such as requiring certain groups of employees who look or sound “foreign” to present particular documents not required of other employees.

Retaliation:

Retaliation occurs when an employer intimidates, threatens, coerces, or otherwise takes negative action against an individual because they have filed an immigration-related employment discrimination charge or complaint; has testified or participated in any immigration-related employment discrimination investigation, proceeding, or hearing; or otherwise asserts their rights under the anti-discrimination provision.

Dealing with Common Interview Problems

Here are common interview problems followed by tips to address them:

Problem: The candidate's answers are too brief.

Solution: While brief answers are sometimes appropriate during an interview, when a candidate is too brief, they may not be giving you the information you need to make a valuable assessment of their skills and experience. To encourage a candidate to elaborate, ask them to explain why they answered a question in a particular way. Or try using the “echo” technique: simply repeat what the candidate has just said, but use an inquisitive tone. For example, if a candidate says, “I left my last job because it didn't challenge me,” say, "You left because it didn't challenge you?" The candidate should recognize that you would like more information. You can also use non-verbal signals to encourage a candidate to say more. Nod your head as the candidate answers your questions and allow an occasional silence to follow a response. The candidate is likely to naturally fill the gap with more information.

Problem: The candidate's answers are long-winded.

Solution: By contrast, you may meet an interviewee who rambles, especially when asked open-ended questions. To address this, ask more targeted questions. For instance, instead of asking: “tell me about yourself,” try asking the candidate to describe an average day in their current or previous role, or about their experience and background as it relates to the position for which they are applying.

Problem: The candidate seems to be evading a question.

Solution: In some cases, a candidate may avoid the question entirely. If a candidate fails to answer an important question, ask the question again. If they still do not answer, try asking the question in a different way. For instance, if you ask the candidate to share a job-related weakness, but they only share positive attributes, try re-wording the question. Ask the candidate instead to share some areas they would like to focus on from a professional development standpoint.

Problem: The interviewer is doing most of talking.

Solution: During an interview, allow the candidate to do most of the talking. At the start of the interview, give the candidate a brief overview of the company and the position, and then dive right into your list of prepared questions. Work with co-workers, managers, or human resources to develop a strong list of open-ended and targeted questions to ask each candidate. When the candidate asks questions, keep your answers brief and to the point.

Problem: The candidate's responses seem canned.

Solution: Some candidates may seem like they are only telling you what they think you want to hear. If a candidate seems overly prepared and has rehearsed responses, try asking hypothetical questions to elicit more candid answers. For instance, present the candidate with a scenario they might face in the prospective job and ask them how they would handle it.

Problem: The candidate uses “we” when answering questions.

Solution: If a candidate answers a question by using “we” instead of “I,” they may be referring to work completed with other team members, or overstating their accomplishments. In such cases, ask follow-up questions. For example, if a candidate says: "we increased sales by 20 percent," you could ask how the candidate specifically contributed to increasing sales.
